This is my Grand Challenges Scholars Program portfolio. The NAE Grand Challenges Scholars Program is a national program where engineering students work across five competencies (Talent, Multidisciplinary, Entrepreneurship, Multicultural, and Social Consciousness) to prepare themselves for the biggest problems out there. This portfolio is my record of that work, all tied together by my theme of Security.

I'm interested in the theme Security. GCSP, to me, is a community of people who are passionate about applying our technical skills and analytical processes into making this world a better place. LOVE &
REASON

When I look back at my time in GCSP, the thing that strikes me is how Security kept showing up everywhere, and not just in the way I originally expected. I came in thinking about digital security, encryption, firewalls. I'm leaving with a much wider view: safety, trust, who gets access to what, and why.

My FURI research on ambiguity in Large Language Models was where this started clicking. LLMs hallucinate. They get confused by the same ambiguities that confuse humans, except they do it confidently and at scale. That work turned into an IEEE BigData paper (20+ citations now), and it confirmed something for me: AI safety is what I want to spend my career on.

FSE150 changed how I think about problems. The PESTLE framework forced me to look at engineering solutions from political, economic, social, legal, and environmental angles, not just technical ones. Then STS 330 took that further by examining how AI and data tracking are reshaping labor markets, democracies, and privacy. These are security problems, even if they don't look like traditional cybersecurity. HON 272 and ASB 378 changed how I think about who my work actually affects. And Venture Devils pushed me to ask whether the things I build will last beyond a demo.

I'm grateful for this program, for Professor Trowbridge and Professor Liu and everyone else who pushed me, and for the other scholars who care about this stuff as much as I do. GCSP taught me that good engineering isn't just about writing good code. It's about understanding the world your code lives in. That's what I'm taking with me.
